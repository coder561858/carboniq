📚 Basics

What is a digital carbon footprint?

A website's carbon footprint is the total CO₂ emitted when someone loads a page — from data centers to networks to the device in your hand.

⚡
Every interaction uses energy ▼

Every time someone visits a website, data is transferred across the internet. This data travels through networks, is processed by servers in data centers, and rendered on the user's device.

Each step in this chain consumes electricity. The carbon intensity of that electricity depends on the energy grid powering the infrastructure — coal-heavy grids produce significantly more CO₂ than renewable ones.

📊
Page weight is the biggest factor ▼

The total size of a web page — images, scripts, stylesheets, fonts, and videos — is the primary driver of its carbon footprint. Larger pages require more data transfer, more processing power, and therefore more energy.

The median web page in 2024 is about 2.3 MB. Reducing this by even 30% can significantly lower emissions, especially for high-traffic sites.

🌿
Green hosting makes a massive difference ▼

If your hosting provider runs on renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro), the data center portion of your website's emissions drops by up to 95%. This is verified through the Green Web Foundation's database.

Many major providers like Google Cloud, AWS (some regions), and Cloudflare are already verified green hosts. Check if yours is too!

🌍 Impact

Why reducing emissions matters

A greener web isn't just good ethics — it improves user experience, reduces costs, and builds trust.

🌊

Protect Oceans

Rising CO₂ levels cause ocean acidification, threatening marine ecosystems, coral reefs, and the fishing communities that depend on them.

🌡️

Reduce Temperatures

Digital infrastructure contributes to global warming. Optimizing websites reduces overall energy demand and emissions from the tech sector.

🌲

Forest Health

Every kg of CO₂ saved helps protect forests and ecosystems that serve as vital carbon sinks for the planet.

🔋

Reduce Energy Use

Lighter pages and efficient code mean less electricity consumed by data centers, networks, and user devices worldwide.

🚀

Better SEO

Google rewards fast, lightweight sites. Reducing page weight improves Core Web Vitals and search engine rankings.

🤝

Build Trust

Demonstrating environmental responsibility strengthens your brand, attracting eco-conscious customers and partners.

🔬 Analysis

What carboniq analyzes

A comprehensive breakdown of your website's environmental impact.

📦

Total page size

Complete transfer size including all resources.

We load your page in a real Chromium browser and measure the actual encoded data length of every network request — giving you the true transfer size, including compression.
🖼️

Resource count

Images, scripts, stylesheets, fonts, and media.

Every network request is categorized: images (PNG, JPG, WebP, SVG), JavaScript files, CSS stylesheets, fonts (WOFF2, TTF), media, and other resources. Bloated resource counts slow your page and increase energy use.
⚡

Render-blocking resources

JS and CSS that delay page rendering.

We detect scripts in the <head> without async or defer attributes, and stylesheets that block rendering. These make users wait longer, keeping devices powered on and consuming more energy.
🌐

Server location

IP address mapped to country and region.

We resolve your domain's IP address and geolocate it to a city, region, and country. Server location affects carbon intensity since different electrical grids vary in how much CO₂ they produce per kWh.
🌿

Green hosting check

Is your server powered by renewable energy?

We check the Green Web Foundation's database to verify if your hosting provider uses renewable energy. Green hosting can reduce data center emissions by up to 95%, making it the single most impactful change.
🏆

Carbon score & grade

A+ to F rating based on emissions per visit.

Using the Sustainable Web Design Model v4, we calculate grams of CO₂ per page view and assign a letter grade from A+ (exceptional, under 0.1g) to F (very poor, over 2g), with energy breakdowns across data centers, networks, and user devices.
